## 7-DAY TEST ANALYSIS

### What Was Measured

- Peak Load (compression): 273 psi
- Specimen Type: ASTM C39 cylinder (4" x 8")
- Condition: STILL WET (not oven-dried)
- Failure Mode: Compressed to peak, sides cracked

### Critical Insight: WET vs DRY

**The specimen was tested WET (water still in pores)**

Water in pores reduces friction between particles and acts as a lubricant (weakens the concrete).

Typical WET-to-DRY strength ratio for lightweight concrete:
- WET strength = 80% of DRY strength
- DRY strength = WET / 0.80

### Correction to Equivalent Dry Strength

| Measurement | Value |
|-------------|-------|
| Measured (WET) | 273 psi |
| Converted to equivalent DRY | 273 / 0.80 = **341 psi** |

### Why 273 psi is NORMAL for Mix 3

Mix contains **47.2% slag cement**, which is a **slow-gaining material**.

| Timeframe | Slag Cement | Portland Cement |
|-----------|------------|----------------|
| 7 days | ~60% of ultimate | ~75% of ultimate |
| 14 days | ~85% of ultimate | ~90% of ultimate |
| 28 days | ~100% of ultimate | ~100% of ultimate |

**RESULT:** 7-day result is LOW because slag doesn't develop early strength, but continues STRONG growth through 28 days.

This is **EXACTLY WHAT YOU WANT** for a competition canoe:
- Light on early effort (easy to handle, less cost)
- Strong by 28 days (ready for competition)

## IMPORTANT NOTES

### 1. Wet Specimen Testing is OK

- Acceptable for development testing
- Actually more conservative (lower number)
- But MUST note this in report
- For official ASCE submission: Recommend testing dry specimens (ASTM C39 standard)

### 2. Slag Takes Time (This is GOOD)

- Slower early strength (but that's fine)
- Stronger long-term (perfect for competition date)
- More durable (less permeability, better for water resistance)
- Don't try to speed it up by adding more Portland
