Phase 1: Mold Construction

EPS Foam Female Mold — The Foundation of Your Hull

The mold defines the hull shape. Every imperfection in the mold transfers directly to the concrete shell. A well-built mold with smooth, fair curves is the single most important factor in producing a competitive canoe. The female mold approach (concave, laying concrete inside) is standard for ASCE competition canoes because it produces a smoother exterior surface.

1.3 Cutting Foam Templates — Manual Method

If CNC hot-wire cutting is not available, templates can be cut manually with excellent results. This is the method most student teams actually use. It takes longer but requires no specialized equipment.

Step 1.3.1: Create Cardboard Master Templates [click to check]

  1. Print the cross-section profile for each unique station at 1:1 scale (use a large-format printer or tile across multiple sheets)
  2. For each station: draw a horizontal line = Beam width, centered. Draw vertical lines at each end = Depth (17" for inner hull, accounting for shell thickness). Connect the bottom with the U-profile (flat bottom + 90° walls for our design)
  3. Transfer the profile onto heavy cardboard or 1/4" MDF using carbon paper or spray adhesive
  4. Cut with a jigsaw or utility knife. Label each template with its station number
  5. Sand edges smooth — these are your cutting guides

Step 1.3.2: Transfer Profiles to Foam [click to check]

  1. Lay a foam block flat on a clean work surface
  2. Place the cardboard template on top, aligning the centerline
  3. Trace around the template with a fine-tip Sharpie
  4. Flip the template and trace the mirror side (if asymmetric station)
  5. Mark the station number and orientation arrow on the foam

Step 1.3.3: Cut Foam Profiles [click to check]

  1. Hot-wire method (preferred): Stretch NiChrome wire between a wooden bow frame. Connect to a 12V variable power supply. Adjust current until wire glows barely orange (~400°F). Guide the wire along the traced line at a steady 1–2 inches per second. The wire melts through EPS cleanly with minimal dust.
  2. Hand-saw method: Use a long serrated bread knife or a drywall saw. Cut along the outside of the Sharpie line (leave material to sand). Use long, smooth strokes — short choppy cuts leave ridges.
  3. Band-saw method: If available, a band saw with a fine-tooth blade cuts EPS quickly. Feed slowly to prevent melting and re-welding.
  4. Cut 1/8" outside the line in all cases — you will sand to final profile
SAFETY: Hot-wire cutting produces styrene fumes. Always work outdoors or with strong ventilation. Wear a respirator with organic vapor cartridges. Never use an open flame to cut EPS — it is flammable.

Step 1.3.4: Sand to Final Profile [click to check]

  1. Use a Surform rasp for initial shaping — removes material quickly without tearing
  2. Switch to 80-grit sandpaper on a flat sanding block for fairness
  3. Check against the cardboard template frequently — light behind the template shows gaps
  4. Final pass with 120-grit for smoothness
  5. Tolerance: ±1/16" from the template profile

1.4 CNC Hot-Wire Method (If Available)

If your school has access to a CNC hot-wire cutter (common in architecture and aerospace departments), the process is dramatically faster and more precise. The CNC controller reads G-code generated from the hull coordinates and drives the wire through the foam automatically.

Step 1.4.1: Generate G-Code from Calculator [click to check]

  1. Export station coordinates from concrete_canoe_calculator.py (the station schedule above)
  2. Convert each cross-section profile to a 2D DXF or G-code path
  3. Each station is one cut: the wire traces the U-profile from one side of the foam block to the other
  4. Set wire speed to 1.5"/sec for 2 lb/ft³ EPS, wire temp ~400°F

Step 1.4.2: CNC Cutting Process [click to check]

  1. Secure the foam block to the CNC bed with pins or double-sided tape
  2. Run a dry pass (wire off) to verify the path clears the foam and bed
  3. Power on the wire, run the cut. One station takes ~30 seconds
  4. Remove the cut piece, label it, stack for assembly
  5. CNC tolerance: ±1/32" — far better than manual cutting

1.5 Strongback Assembly

Step 1.5.1: Build the Strongback [click to check]

  1. The strongback is a flat, rigid base that holds all station templates in alignment. Use 3/4" MDF or plywood, 36" wide × 196" long (hull + 2" margin each end)
  2. If a single 16-ft sheet isn't available, join two sheets with a butt joint reinforced by a 12" splice plate screwed to the underside
  3. Snap a centerline down the full length with a chalk line
  4. Mark station positions every 6" starting from one end (32 marks total)
  5. At each station mark, drill 3/8" holes for alignment dowels

Step 1.5.2: Mount Station Templates [click to check]

  1. Drill matching 3/8" holes in the bottom of each foam station template (2 holes, symmetric about centerline)
  2. Apply polyurethane adhesive to the bottom face of each template
  3. Press each template onto the strongback at its marked position, pushing dowels through both holes
  4. Secure with 2.5" drywall screws from below (pre-drill through MDF)
  5. Check vertical alignment with a level — templates must be plumb
  6. Check fore-aft alignment by sighting along the sheer line (top edge) — should be a smooth curve with no bumps

Step 1.5.3: Fill Between Stations [click to check]

  1. Cut filler blocks from scrap EPS to fit roughly between each pair of adjacent stations
  2. Glue filler blocks in place with polyurethane adhesive. Clamp or weight until cured (2–4 hrs)
  3. The filler doesn't need to be precise — you'll shape it in the fairing step
  4. Leave 1/4" proud (above the template surface) to ensure full coverage for sanding

1.6 Fairing the Mold

Step 1.6.1: Rough Fairing [click to check]

  1. Use a Surform rasp to knock down high spots between stations. Work longitudinally (along the hull length), not across it
  2. Use a long flexible batten (thin wood strip or aluminum bar, 36–48" long) to check fairness: hold the batten against the mold surface along the length. It should make contact along its full length with no gaps > 1/16"
  3. Mark high spots with a Sharpie, sand them down, re-check with the batten
  4. Work from the center of the hull outward toward bow and stern

Step 1.6.2: Fine Fairing & Surface Prep [click to check]

  1. Switch to 80-grit on a large sanding board (12" or longer). Sand the entire surface, blending station-to-station transitions
  2. Progress through 120 and 220 grit for the final surface. The mold surface should feel smooth and continuous
  3. Fill any remaining low spots with lightweight spackle or Bondo, then sand flush
  4. Vacuum all dust thoroughly — any dust under the release agent will transfer to the hull

Step 1.6.3: Apply Mold Release Agent [click to check]

  1. Apply 2 coats of PVA mold release (Partall Film #10 or equivalent) with a foam brush
  2. First coat: thin, even, let dry 30 minutes until clear and tacky
  3. Second coat: perpendicular to first coat direction, let dry 1 hour
  4. Do NOT touch the mold surface after release is applied — fingerprints break the release film
  5. Optional: apply paste wax before PVA for extra insurance on porous foam
Pro Tip: Some teams seal the EPS foam with a coat of water-based polyurethane before applying PVA release. This prevents the foam from absorbing the release agent and gives a glassier mold surface.

Phase 2: Concrete Mix Design

60 PCF Lightweight Concrete — Batch Planning & Testing

The concrete mix must achieve a unit weight of 60 lb/ft³ (960 kg/m³) while maintaining a compressive strength of at least f'c = 2000 psi and a modulus of rupture f_r = 1500 psi. This is accomplished using lightweight aggregates (Poraver expanded glass or K1 cenospheres) to replace normal-weight sand and gravel, combined with PVA fiber reinforcement and a carefully controlled water-to-cite ratio.

2.2 Mixing Procedure

Step 2.2.1: Pre-Wet Aggregates [click to check]

  1. Poraver beads are highly absorptive. Pre-soak in water for 24 hours, then drain for 30 minutes
  2. This prevents the aggregates from stealing water from the cement paste during hydration
  3. Weigh aggregates after soaking — adjust mix water to compensate for absorbed water

Step 2.2.2: Dry Mix [click to check]

  1. In a clean 5-gallon bucket, combine cement, pre-wetted Poraver (both sizes), and K1 cenospheres
  2. Mix dry ingredients for 2 minutes with a drill-mounted paddle mixer at low speed
  3. Add PVA fibers gradually while mixing to prevent clumping

Step 2.2.3: Add Water & Admixtures [click to check]

  1. Add 80% of the mix water. Mix for 2 minutes at medium speed
  2. Add superplasticizer (HRWR) to the remaining 20% water, stir to dissolve
  3. Add the HRWR-water mixture. Mix for 3 more minutes
  4. Target consistency: thick pancake batter — should hold shape when scooped but flow when vibrated
  5. If too stiff, add water in 1-oz increments. If too wet, add a pinch of cement. Record adjustments!

Step 2.2.4: Test & Document [click to check]

  1. Measure unit weight: fill a known-volume container (e.g., 1-quart deli container), weigh, calculate PCF. Target: 52–62 PCF
  2. Cast 3 test cylinders (2"×4" or 3"×6") for 7-day and 28-day compression tests
  3. Record: date, batch number, all ingredient weights, water added, slump/flow, unit weight, ambient temperature
  4. Photo the consistency and test cylinders for documentation
SAFETY: Portland cement is highly alkalite (pH 12–13). Always wear nitrile gloves and safety glasses when mixing. Wash skin contact immediately. Wear an N95 mask when handling dry cement — silica dust causes silicosis.

Phase 3: Concrete Layup

Three-Layer Application with Basalt Mesh Reinforcement

The layup is the most critical construction day. The entire shell must be placed in a single continuous session (6–10 hours) to ensure monolithic bonding between layers. Plan for at least 8 people: 2 mixing, 4 applying, 1 checking thickness, 1 managing tools and materials. Run through the plan as a team the day before.

Target shell thickness: 0.75" (19mm) total, applied in three layers:
• Layer 1: ~0.25" (scratch coat + first mesh)
• Layer 2: ~0.25" (fill coat + second mesh)
• Layer 3: ~0.25" (finish coat, trowel smooth)

3.1 Reinforcement Preparation

Step 3.1.1: Cut Basalt Mesh [click to check]

  1. Total mesh needed: 55.5 ft² (2 layers × 27.7 ft² each)
  2. Cut mesh into manageable panels: bottom panel (full length × beam width) and side panels (full length × wall height)
  3. Pre-cut dart relief cuts at the bow and stern where the mesh must conform to compound curves
  4. Label each panel (L1-Bottom, L1-Left, L1-Right, L2-Bottom, L2-Left, L2-Right)
  5. Verify mesh POA (percent open area) ≥ 40% per ASCE rules
  6. Wear gloves — basalt fiber ends are sharp

3.2 Layer 1 — Scratch Coat

Step 3.2.1: Apply Scratch Coat [click to check]

  1. Verify mold release is intact (no fingerprints, scratches, or dust)
  2. Mix Batch 1. Begin at the bottom center of the mold
  3. Apply concrete with a small flexible trowel or gloved hands. Press firmly into the mold surface
  4. Work outward from center, up the sides. Target thickness: 1/8" to 3/16"
  5. The scratch coat must be continuous — no dry spots or gaps

Step 3.2.2: Place First Mesh Layer [click to check]

  1. While the scratch coat is still wet, lay the bottom mesh panel into the mold
  2. Press the mesh gently into the wet concrete with a roller or flat trowel
  3. Place side panels, overlapping bottom panel by at least 2"
  4. Ensure mesh is fully embedded — no mesh should be visible on the mold-side surface
  5. At bow and stern, overlap dart cuts by 1" minimum

Step 3.2.3: Consolidate Layer 1 [click to check]

  1. Apply another 1/8" of concrete over the mesh, pressing through the openings
  2. Use a small vibrating tool (palm sander without sandpaper, or a vibrating phone taped to a trowel) to consolidate — 5–10 seconds per area
  3. Total Layer 1 thickness: ~0.25" with mesh embedded in the middle
  4. Let Layer 1 set for 30–45 minutes until it can support light finger pressure without deforming

3.3 Layer 2 — Fill Coat

Step 3.3.1: Apply Fill Coat & Second Mesh [click to check]

  1. Mist Layer 1 surface lightly with water (spray bottle) to improve bonding
  2. Mix Batches 3–5. Apply concrete ~1/8" thick over the entire Layer 1 surface
  3. Place second mesh layer (same procedure as Layer 1)
  4. Apply another ~1/8" of concrete over the second mesh
  5. Consolidate with vibration. Total cumulative thickness: ~0.50"

3.4 Layer 3 — Finish Coat

Step 3.4.1: Apply Finish Coat [click to check]

  1. Mist Layer 2 surface lightly
  2. Mix Batches 6–8. Apply the final ~0.25" layer
  3. This is the interior surface of the canoe — trowel it as smooth as possible
  4. Use a steel finishing trowel for the final passes. Wet the trowel to prevent dragging

Step 3.4.2: Thickness Verification [click to check]

  1. Before the concrete sets, check thickness at 20+ points using a depth gauge (thin wire with a stop)
  2. Push the wire through to the mold surface, read the depth
  3. Target: 0.75" ± 0.0625" (3/4" ± 1/16")
  4. Add material where thin, remove where thick. Document measurements on a hull map
  5. Pay special attention to the chines (bottom-to-side corners) and gunwale areas — these tend to be thin

Step 3.4.3: Gunwale Formation [click to check]

  1. Build up the gunwale (top edge) to 1.0–1.25" thickness for structural reinforcement
  2. Form a smooth, rounded profile on the top edge — sharp edges crack
  3. The gunwale takes the most handling abuse; it needs to be the strongest part
CRITICAL TIMING: All 3 layers must be placed within 4–6 hours. If Layer 1 fully sets before Layer 2 is placed, you get a cold joint — a weak plane that can delaminate under load. Keep the previous layer damp if there's a delay.

Phase 4: Curing

28-Day Wet Cure Protocol — ACI 308R Compliant

Curing is the chemical hydration process where cement reacts with water to form calcium silicate hydrate (C-S-H) gel — the "glue" that gives concrete its strength. If the concrete dries out prematurely, hydration stops and you get a weak, crumbly shell. Proper curing is non-negotiable. Per ACI 308R, moist curing must continue for a minimum of 7 days, but 28 days is standard for competition canoes to achieve full design strength.

Step 4.1: Initial Set (0–24 hours) [click to check]

  1. Immediately after finishing the layup, cover the entire mold with damp burlap or cotton towels
  2. Wrap the entire mold in 6-mil polyethylene sheeting (painter's plastic), sealing edges with tape
  3. The goal is to create a humid chamber — no moisture should escape
  4. Do NOT move, bump, or vibrate the mold for at least 12 hours
  5. Temperature should be 60–80°F. If curing outdoors in winter, use insulation blankets or a heated enclosure

Step 4.2: Daily Maintenance (Days 1–28) [click to check]

  1. Check the plastic wrap daily. Look for condensation on the inside — this means moisture is present (good)
  2. If the burlap feels dry, re-wet it with a spray bottle. The concrete surface should never appear dry
  3. Re-seal any plastic that has come loose
  4. Log the date, temperature, and moisture condition in a curing log

Step 4.3: Strength Testing Schedule [click to check]

  1. Day 7: Break 1 test cylinder (compression test per ASTM C39). Should reach ~70% of 28-day strength (≥1400 psi)
  2. Day 14: Break 1 test cylinder. Should reach ~85% (≥1700 psi)
  3. Day 28: Break final cylinder. Must reach f'c = 2000 psi
  4. If 7-day strength is below 1200 psi, investigate: was the mix correct? Is the cure environment wet enough?

Phase 5: Demolding

Mechanical Separation — The Most Nerve-Wracking Day

Demolding is the process of separating the cured concrete shell from the foam mold. This is the highest-risk step in the entire build: one wrong move can crack or break the hull. The PVA release agent creates a boundary layer between foam and concrete, but mechanical force is still needed to overcome suction and adhesion. Plan this carefully and rehearse with the whole team.

Step 5.1: Prepare for Demolding [click to check]

  1. Assemble the full team (minimum 6 people for lifting)
  2. Clear and pad the receiving area — lay foam mats, blankets, or cardboard where the hull will rest
  3. Build or acquire two padded sawhorses or cradles to support the hull upright
  4. Have repair materials ready: rapid-set cement, epoxy, mesh patches
  5. Remove all plastic sheeting and burlap from the mold

Step 5.2: Break the Bond [click to check]

  1. Start at the gunwales. Insert thin plastic wedges (cut from old credit cards or plastic shims) between the concrete and foam at the top edge
  2. Work wedges along the entire gunwale perimeter, gently tapping with a rubber mallet
  3. Listen for a "cracking" sound — this is the PVA release breaking, NOT the concrete (usually). A dull thud means the concrete is still bonded; a sharp pop means release
  4. Once the gunwale is released on both sides, work wedges down the sides
  5. For the bottom, compressed air can help: insert an air nozzle at the gunwale gap and apply low-pressure (10–15 psi) air to break suction

Step 5.3: Lift and Separate [click to check]

  1. With the bond broken, position 3 people on each side of the mold, hands under the gunwale lip
  2. On a count of three, lift the hull straight up, keeping it level. Do NOT tilt or flex
  3. Lift at least 6" above the mold, then walk it to the padded receiving area
  4. Set down gently onto the cradles, exterior (smooth mold-side) facing down
  5. If any foam remains bonded to the hull exterior, do NOT pry it — dissolve with acetone or sand it off gently

Step 5.4: Post-Demold Inspection [click to check]

  1. Visually inspect the entire hull for cracks, voids, delamination, or thin spots
  2. Tap the surface with a coin — a clear ring means solid concrete; a dull thud means a void or delamination
  3. Measure wall thickness at 20+ points with calipers or an ultrasonic thickness gauge
  4. Photograph all defects with a ruler for scale. Map their locations on a hull diagram
  5. Small cracks (< 1/16" wide) and voids (< 1" dia) can be repaired. Structural cracks or delamination over large areas may require a rebuild
Pro Tip: Some teams sacrifice the foam mold by dissolving it with acetone or gasoline after demolding (EPS dissolves in organic solvents). This works but destroys the mold. If you might need to re-cast, preserve the mold by using the mechanical method.

Phase 6: Finishing

Patching, Sanding, Sealing, Painting & Hardware

Finishing transforms a rough concrete shell into a race-ready canoe. This is the most labor-intensive phase, typically consuming 100–300 person-hours of sanding alone. The quality of the finish directly affects both aesthetics (judging points) and hydrodynamic performance (surface drag). Start early and be patient.

Step 6.1: Patch Defects [click to check]

  1. Mix a small batch of patching compound: Portland cement + fine sand (1:1) + acrylic bonding agent + water to a stiff paste
  2. Wet the repair area. Pack patching compound into voids and over cracks. Trowel flush with the surrounding surface
  3. For larger repairs: cut a mesh patch 2" larger than the defect, embed in the patching compound
  4. Moist-cure patches for 3–7 days under damp rags

Step 6.2: Sanding Schedule [click to check]

  1. 80-grit: Remove major high spots, shape the gunwale profile, blend patches. Use an orbital sander or sanding block. This is the roughest and most aggressive step
  2. 120-grit: Smooth the 80-grit scratches. The hull should feel uniformly textured, no bumps or ridges
  3. 220-grit: Refine the surface. At this point, run your hand over the hull — it should feel like smooth stone
  4. 400-grit (wet): Wet-sand the exterior for a near-glass finish. This step is critical for hydrodynamics
  5. Between each grit: wipe down with a damp cloth to remove all dust
  6. Wear an N95 respirator for ALL sanding — concrete dust contains crystalline silica

Step 6.3: Sealing [click to check]

  1. Apply a penetrating concrete sealer (silane/siloxane-based, e.g., Chemique H&C or SikaGuard) to reduce water absorption
  2. Apply 2 coats by brush or roller, 4 hours apart. The sealer soaks into the concrete pores
  3. Alternative: marine-grade epoxy coat (West System 105/205) for a completely waterproof barrier. Apply 2–3 thin coats, sanding between with 220-grit
  4. Let cure 24–48 hours before painting

Step 6.4: Painting & Graphics [click to check]

  1. Apply 1 coat of primer (concrete/masonry primer) and let dry
  2. Apply 2–3 coats of exterior latex or marine paint in team colors. Light colors recommended for visibility on water
  3. For graphics/logos: use vinyl stencils or vinyl decals. Some teams use automotive clear-coat for a glossy finish
  4. Reminder: ASCE rules require the canoe name, university name, and competition year to be visible on the hull

Step 6.5: Hardware Installation [click to check]

  1. Seats: Install 2 seats at positions per ASCE Sec 6.2 spacing requirements. Attach with marine adhesive (3M 5200) and stainless steel bolts through pre-drilled holes with rubber washers
  2. Carrying handles: Nylon rope through drilled holes at bow and stern, reinforced with epoxy-filled backing plates
  3. Keel strip (optional): Apply a thin bead of marine epoxy along the bottom centerline for abrasion protection
  4. Flotation: ASCE requires positive flotation. Secure closed-cell foam blocks under each seat

Step 6.6: Final Weigh-In & Float Test [click to check]

  1. Weigh the completed hull on a calibrated scale. Record: bare hull weight, weight with hardware
  2. Compare to calculated weight (245 lbs target). Tolerance: ±15%
  3. Float test: place the canoe in water (pool or calm lake). Check for leaks, measure actual draft and freeboard
  4. Load test: add crew weight (sandbags) per each load case (2-person men, 4-person coed, etc.). Measure draft and freeboard under load. Verify freeboard ≥ 6" per ASCE rules
  5. Document everything with photos and measurements for the design report

Safety Requirements

PPE, Chemical Hazards & Workshop Protocols

Chemical Hazards

  • Portland cement: pH 12–13, causes chemical burns. Wear nitrile gloves, wash immediately on skin contact
  • Concrete dust: Contains crystalline silica (OSHA PEL 50 μg/m³). N95 minimum; P100 half-face for extended sanding
  • Styrene fumes: From hot-wire cutting EPS. Organic vapor cartridge respirator required
  • Epoxy resins: Skin sensitizer. Nitrile gloves + long sleeves. Once sensitized, reaction is permanent
  • Superplasticizer: Mild irritant. Gloves when handling concentrate

Physical Hazards

  • Lifting: The hull weighs ~245 lbs. Minimum 6 people for any lift. Bend at the knees, not the waist
  • Power tools: Eye protection (ANSI Z87.1 safety glasses) mandatory. Hearing protection for extended sanding
  • Hot wire: Burns at 400°F. Keep clear of skin, hair, and clothing. Unplug when not cutting
  • Tripping: Keep work area clean. Coil extension cords. Store tools when not in use
  • EPS ignition: EPS is flammable (melts at 464°F, ignites at 680°F). No open flames in the work area

Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

Mold Mistakes

  • Unfair surface: Causes bumps in the hull. Solution: use a long batten to check fairness constantly
  • Skipping mold release: Hull bonds permanently to foam. Apply 2 coats PVA, no exceptions
  • Misaligned stations: Creates a crooked hull. Use alignment dowels and check with string line

Mix Mistakes

  • Too much water: Reduces strength by 50%+ per Abrams' law. Use superplasticizer for workability, NOT water
  • Not pre-soaking Poraver: Aggregate steals mix water, causes dry crumbly concrete
  • Inconsistent batches: Weigh every ingredient on a scale. Never estimate

Layup Mistakes

  • Cold joints: Waiting too long between layers. Keep within 4–6 hours, mist interface
  • Uneven thickness: Check with depth gauge at 20+ points during layup
  • Mesh not embedded: Exposed mesh = corrosion path. Press mesh fully into wet concrete

Curing Mistakes

  • Letting it dry out: Even one day of dry curing can reduce strength by 30%. Keep it wrapped and wet
  • Demolding too early: Wait minimum 14 days, ideally 28. Early demold = cracking under self-weight
  • Temperature extremes: Below 50°F, hydration nearly stops. Use insulation in cold weather

Demolding Mistakes

  • Prying with metal tools: Gouges and cracks the shell. Use plastic wedges only
  • Tilting during lift: Creates bending stress that cracks the hull. Lift straight up, keep level
  • Dropping: Even a 2" drop can crack a concrete canoe. Pad everything, move slowly

Finishing Mistakes

  • Sanding too aggressively: Exposes mesh or thins the shell. Use light pressure, let the paper do the work
  • Skipping grits: Going from 80 straight to 400 takes forever. Follow the sequence: 80→120→220→400
  • Painting over dust: Paint peels. Wipe with damp cloth and let dry before each paint coat
